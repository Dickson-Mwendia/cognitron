## Digital Safety Is a Parenting Issue

Most Nairobi parents lock their doors at night, know their children's friends, and monitor where they go after school. But how many apply the same vigilance to the digital spaces where their children spend hours every day?

The internet gives children access to the entire world — and the entire world access to your children. Digital safety isn't a "tech issue" to delegate to your IT department at work. It's a parenting responsibility, and it's more urgent than most families realise.

## The Kenyan Digital Landscape

Kenya has one of the highest internet penetration rates in Africa at over 85%. Mobile is king — most Kenyan children access the internet primarily through smartphones, often shared family devices. Social media usage among teenagers is near-universal, with WhatsApp, TikTok, Instagram, and Snapchat dominating.

This creates specific risks:

- **Shared devices** mean children can access adult content or communications not intended for them
- **Mobile-first access** means less parental visibility compared to a family computer in the living room
- **Rapid social media adoption** often outpaces parents' understanding of the platforms
- **Online financial fraud** targeting M-Pesa and mobile banking affects families, including through children who share information unknowingly

## Five Practical Steps for Every Family

### 1. Secure Your Devices

Start with the basics — they matter more than any parental control software:

- **Set up separate user profiles** for children on shared devices. Both Android and iOS support this. A child profile limits which apps are accessible and prevents access to your banking and messaging apps.
- **Enable built-in parental controls.** Google Family Link (Android) and Screen Time (iOS) are free, effective, and take 15 minutes to set up.
- **Use strong, unique passwords** for your email, M-Pesa, and social media accounts. If your child knows your phone PIN, they can access everything.
- **Keep software updated.** Those update notifications aren't just annoying — they patch security vulnerabilities that criminals actively exploit.

### 2. Know the Age Limits

Every major social media platform has a minimum age requirement, typically 13 years:

- **WhatsApp:** 16 years (often ignored in Kenya)
- **TikTok:** 13 years
- **Instagram:** 13 years
- **Snapchat:** 13 years
- **YouTube:** 13 years (YouTube Kids is available for younger children)

These limits exist for legal and developmental reasons. A 10-year-old on TikTok isn't just breaking terms of service — they're being exposed to content algorithms designed for adults, with no age-appropriate filtering.

If your child is under 13, they shouldn't be on social media. If they're 13–16, they should be on monitored, agreed-upon platforms only.

### 3. Have the Conversations

Technology changes constantly, but the principles of staying safe don't. Have ongoing conversations about:

- **Personal information:** Never share your full name, school, home address, or daily routine with people you've only met online. This includes gaming friends and social media followers.
- **Stranger danger — digital version:** Anyone online can pretend to be anyone. An "11-year-old chess friend" might not be 11 or a chess player.
- **Screenshots are forever:** Anything sent digitally — messages, photos, videos — can be saved, shared, and forwarded. The delete button is an illusion.
- **When to tell a parent:** If anyone online makes them uncomfortable, asks for photos, wants to meet in person, or threatens them — tell a parent immediately, without fear of punishment.

### 4. Monitor Without Smothering

There's a balance between safety and trust. Heavy-handed surveillance can damage your relationship and drive children to find workarounds. A better approach:

- **Keep devices in common areas** during primary school years (no phones in bedrooms at night)
- **Follow your child on social media** — not to spy, but to understand the platforms they use
- **Weekly check-ins** — "Show me something cool you saw online this week" is a non-threatening way to stay connected
- **Review app permissions together** — make it a collaborative exercise, not an interrogation
- **Respect appropriate privacy** for older teenagers while maintaining agreed-upon boundaries

### 5. Teach Critical Thinking Online

The most dangerous threats online aren't hackers — they're misinformation, manipulation, and social engineering. Teach your child to:

- **Question everything:** "Is this true? How do I know? Who's saying this and why?"
- **Recognise scams:** "If it sounds too good to be true, it is." This applies to free MPESA offers, prize notifications, and "you've won" messages.
- **Understand advertising:** Influencer content is often paid promotion. Teach children to spot #ad and sponsored content.
- **Protect their data:** Every "free" app and quiz collects data. Discuss what data is and why it has value.

## When to Seek Professional Help

If your child has experienced cyberbullying, exposure to inappropriate content, online predatory behaviour, or identity theft, don't handle it alone. Report to:

- **Kenya's Computer Incident Response Team (KE-CIRT):** For cybercrime reporting
- **Childline Kenya (116):** Free helpline for children in distress
- **Your child's school counsellor:** For emotional support and mediation
- **The local police Cybercrime Unit:** For criminal matters
