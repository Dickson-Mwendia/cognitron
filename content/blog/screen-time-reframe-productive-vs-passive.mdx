## The Wrong Question

"How much screen time should my child have?" is the question every parent asks. It's also the wrong question.

Asking about the quantity of screen time is like asking how many hours your child should spend with books. It depends entirely on *what* they're reading. An hour of studying is not the same as an hour of comic books, which is not the same as an hour of reading assignments they don't understand.

The same principle applies to screens. The right question is: **what is my child doing on the screen?**

## Two Types of Screen Time

### Passive Consumption

Passive screen time is when your child is a spectator. They're absorbing content created by someone else, with minimal cognitive engagement:

- Scrolling TikTok or Instagram Reels
- Watching YouTube videos endlessly (especially algorithmic autoplay)
- Consuming social media feeds
- Playing simple, repetitive mobile games designed to be addictive

Passive consumption activates the brain's reward centres (dopamine hits from likes, new content, game rewards) without requiring sustained thinking. Research from the National Institutes of Health found that excessive passive screen time in children is associated with reduced cortical thickness — the brain literally develops differently.

### Active Creation

Active screen time is when your child is a builder. They're using the screen as a tool to create, solve, and think:

- Writing code in Scratch, Python, or JavaScript
- Building a game, website, or animation
- Editing a video or composing music
- Playing chess online against a real opponent
- Researching a topic and writing about it
- Using design tools to create digital art
- Working through structured learning with a coach

Active creation requires planning, problem-solving, persistence through frustration, and original thinking. It builds the same cognitive muscles as traditional "productive" activities like building with Lego or writing an essay.

## The Research Is Clear

A comprehensive study published in *JAMA Pediatrics* (2019) found that the **type** of screen activity mattered far more than the total time. Children who spent time on educational and creative screen activities showed no negative effects on wellbeing or academic performance — even at higher total screen hours.

Meanwhile, children with high levels of passive consumption showed increased rates of anxiety, reduced attention span, and lower academic performance, even when total screen time was moderate.

The American Academy of Pediatrics has moved away from strict hourly limits, instead recommending that families focus on the *quality* of screen activities.

## Practical Tips for Nairobi Parents

### 1. Audit Before You Limit

Before setting time limits, spend a week observing what your child actually does on their device. You might find they spend 30 minutes on something genuinely educational and two hours on autoplay videos. Address the two hours; protect the 30 minutes.

### 2. Create a "Screen Menu"

Just as you wouldn't let your child eat whatever they want, don't let algorithms decide what they consume. Create an approved list of apps and activities:

- **Green list** (unlimited): Coding platforms, chess, educational projects, creative tools
- **Yellow list** (time-limited): Entertainment YouTube, games with educational value
- **Red list** (weekends only or banned): Social media, algorithmic feeds, addictive games

### 3. Co-Create, Don't Just Co-View

Instead of sitting next to your child while they watch YouTube, sit next to them while they build something. Ask questions: "What are you making? How does that work? What happens if you change this?" Your engagement signals that creation is valued.

### 4. Replace, Don't Remove

Telling a child "no more YouTube" without offering an alternative creates conflict. Instead, offer a trade: "Let's try building a game instead of playing one." Children who discover the joy of creation often self-regulate their consumption naturally.

### 5. Model the Behaviour

Children mirror their parents. If you spend your evenings scrolling social media, your child will too. If they see you writing, learning, or building something on your device, they'll associate screens with productivity.
